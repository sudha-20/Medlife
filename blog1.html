MENSTRUAL STIGMA NEEDS TO END. PERIOD.


Menstruation- An important and essential biological function experienced by women around the global population, yet considered a derogatory/dirty word and looked down upon, so much so that it is demonstrated with various different terms like “that time of the month”, “period”, “female trouble” and many more, not to forget these are just in English! Women in different cultures have been made to feel ashamed about menstruation and for quite a lot of us this shame takes the form of embarrassment about leaks, even censorship on social media platforms, but it is not just limited to that. In some areas of the world, due to this stigma, it has costed women their education, careers and even lives for that matter. Menstruation is a topic that usually people are a little uncomfortable discussing about and is talked about only behind closed doors. This is due to the fact that communities have developed detrimental concepts and wrong beliefs about it. And due to these pre-conceived notions, there are notable lack of health education resources available to educate people about menstruation and menstrual cycles. It is the very same lack of facts that adds fuel to the myths and in turn embarrasses women during their monthly cycles.


Back in January 2017, Megan Markle, whom we all know, visited India and witnessed what menstrual stigma could do to adolescent girls. She said and I quote, “Many girls feel ashamed of going to school during their periods, ill equipped with rags instead of pads/cups and they often drop out of the school. Furthermore, many young girls assume that their bodies are purging evil spirits and this is a shame filled reality they silently endure.” This issue is of course not just limited to India. For example, in Venezuela a lot of women are made to sleep in huts during that time of the month. In Ghana, menstruating women are not allowed in their own houses! In Kenya, girls miss an average of 4 days of school each month. Women go through a lot while menstruating- from raging hormones to facing harassment and discrimination.


But it is us who have to come up with this existing issue, if we want to progress further. The easiest task a person can undertake to end menstrual stigma is to not take part in discussing it ambiguously. By creating a dialogue about any topic openly, we assist to remove the societal rule against discoursing it. Even a small dialogue amongst your known group can cause a huge change. India has several policies that tackle awareness about menstruation and menstrual hygiene. Many states have accepted and combined life skills that include inclusive sexuality education into their lower- and upper- secondary school syllabi. Even civil society organisations endorse girls’ education and work with parents and communities to break these stigmas


Thanks to these initiatives, the uneasiness around menstruation and menstrual hygiene is started to gradually fade. It is now widely accepted that girls’ and women’s access to effective means of handling menstrual hygiene is strictly linked to realising their dignity. We must join efforts to break these restrictions that have been built over centuries and are deep-seated in people’s minds. Until we allow young girls to feel “normal” about menstruating, the best-intentioned policies will fall short of achieving the desired goals of equal participation of women and men.
